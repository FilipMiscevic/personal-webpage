Welcome!

Filip Miscevic - I am a recent graduate of the University of Toronto, with a BSc in Computer Science, Neuroscience and Cognitive Science, and a Psychology Minor. I am passionate about a scientific inquiry into the mind and the pursuit of artificial intelligence, and I care deeply about the impact such knowledge may have for humanity. I think we're going to have the most success in both endeavors and best manage their consequences if we listen to what each of the disciplines studying the mind have to say. In line with this vision, I have sought to combine these disciplines in my undergrad and beyond.

Currently, I work at the Computational Linguistics Lab at the University of Toronto, the Centre for Computational Medicine at SickKids Hospital, and the Wisdom and Consciousness Lab at the University of Toronto. I invite you to check out my projects below for more details. Would you like to get in touch with me? Feel free to contact me.

Projects

Computational Linguistics Lab

University of Toronto

It has been observed that networks tracking semantic relationships between words have a scale-free small-world structure, in both naturalistic data (Steyvers and Tenenbaum, 2005) and machine word-learners (Nematzadeh, Fazly and Stevenson, 2014). Moreover, scale-free small-world connectivity appears to be a property of brain networks, and correlates with measures of general intelligence (psychometric g). Drawing on recent work linking small-world networks to the structure of semantic memory (Abbott, Austerweil and Griffiths, 2015), I investigate what it is about this topology that captures human performance on tasks such as word fluency.

Consciousness Studies Lab

University of Toronto

Our central research interest is to better understand and critically evaluate theories of consciousness through experimental work. Wisdom affords a unique opportunity to study consciousness because, historically, practitioners of wisdom traditions experience altered states of consciousness. There has been increasing interest in the way wisdom traditions affect the brain and we seek to better characterize both wisdom and the way it alters brain function.

I also conduct independent research integrating the deep continuity hypothesis - the strong thesis to embodied cognition - with modern neuroscientific work on intelligent problem solving, network connectivity and metastable dynamics of the brain.
