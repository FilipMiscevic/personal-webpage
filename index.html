Welcome!

I am interested in how an understanding of the mind will change our clinical, scientific - and perhaps most importantly - social and cultural practices. I think we are going to have the most success understanding the mind and appropriating its implications if we listen to what each of the disciplines studying the mind have to say. To that end, I find inspiration from computational, neuroscientific and philosophical approaches to brain sciences.

I completed my undergraduate degree at the University of Toronto, with a BSc in Computer Science, Neuroscience and Cognitive Science. Previously, I have worked in the Computational Linguistics Lab and the Consciousness Studies Lab at the University of Toronto, the Centre for Computational Medicine at SickKids Hospital, and the Zebrafish Center for Advanced Drug Discovery at St. Michael's Hospital. I invite you to check out my projects below for more details.

Projects

Computational Linguistics Lab

University of Toronto

It has been observed that networks tracking semantic relationships between words have a small-world structure, in both human-generated data (Steyvers and Tenenbaum, 2005) and machine word-learners (Nematzadeh, Fazly and Stevenson, 2014). Moreover, small-world connectivity appears to be a property of functional brain networks, and correlates with measures of general intelligence (psychometric g). Drawing on recent work (Abbott, Austerweil and Griffiths, 2015), I aim to investigate whether the structure of semantic memory employs the small-world design pattern, and if so, why this may confer advantages in cognitive processing.

Consciousness Studies Lab

University of Toronto

Our central research interest is to better understand consciousness, primarily by investigating altered states of consciousness. Historically, practitioners of wisdom traditions reliably experience altered states of consciousness. Given this link between wisdom and consciousness, we seek to better characterize wisdom and the way it alters brain function.

I also conduct independent research integrating the deep continuity hypothesis - the strong thesis to embodied cognition - with modern neuroscientific work on intelligent problem solving, network connectivity and metastable brain dynamics.
